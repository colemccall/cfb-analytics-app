How Player Ratings Work

A plain-English explanation of the model, its inputs, its weights, and its known limitations.

The core idea

CFB recruiting rankings measure potential — how scouts evaluate a player before they've played a college snap. Our ratings measure production — what a player actually did on the field, relative to every other player at their position in the same season.

A 3-star wide receiver who leads the SEC in yards per reception will rate higher than a 5-star recruit who hasn't lived up to his billing. That gap is the "hidden gem" this platform is designed to find.

Ratings run from 30 to 99. They are relative rankings within a season — a 90-rated QB is not absolutely better than a 90-rated DL; they are each in roughly the top 10% of their position group that year.

Rating tiers

85–99 Elite — top ~10% of the position group
70–84 Great — clear starter, above-average production
55–69 Solid — contributing starter
45–54 Average — rotation or fringe starter
<45 Below — backup or insufficient data

How ratings are calculated

Ratings are built from two data sources depending on the position:

Offensive skill players (QB, RB, WR, TE) are primarily rated on EDGE — our opponent-adjusted, situation-weighted play-by-play metric. Recruiting composite is a 5% anchor only.

Defensive and OL players are rated on season stat composites (tackles, sacks, TFLs, etc.) because play-by-play EPA attribution is not available for these positions. Recruiting composite is included at 15–20%.

What is EDGE?

EDGE (Efficiency-Driven Grade per Event) is our custom player efficiency metric built from raw play-by-play data. It answers: "how much did this player improve his team's scoring odds per play, accounting for who he was playing and when in the game it happened?"

  1. Classify every play by game situation. Crunch time (one-possession game in the 4th quarter) = 2× weight. Garbage time (blowout, 8+ minutes left, period 3+) = 0.25× weight. Normal situations = 1× weight.
  2. Apply opponent quality multiplier. Each play's EPA is scaled by the opponent's SP+ rating. A great play against a top-25 defense counts 1.5×; the same play against a weak defense counts closer to 0.5×. This prevents players on weak schedules from appearing elite.
  3. Sum adjusted EPA, scale by volume. Total weighted EPA is divided by √(plays counted) — this rewards players who are productive over many opportunities, while not harshly penalizing players with fewer snaps.
  4. Blend with season stats and percentile-rank within the position group. EDGE is 55% of the score for skill players; traditional efficiency stats (yards per carry, TD:INT ratio, etc.) fill the remaining 40%. Recruiting composite is the final 5%.
  5. Scale to 30–99 using MinMax scaling within the position group. Players below the starter threshold receive a recruiting-anchored fallback rating — if a player hasn't played, recruiting stars are the best available signal.

What the "Why this rating?" bars mean

When you open a player card, the Why this rating? section shows a bar for each model factor. Each bar represents how much that factor deviated from the median, expressed as a share of total model weight.

  • ▲ green bar — this factor is above the median for the position and raised the rating
  • ▼ red bar — this factor is below the median and pulled the rating down
  • A wider bar means the factor had more influence (either its weight is high, or the player is a strong outlier on that metric)

Example: A QB showing ▲ 28% for Yards/Att and ▼ 18% for TD:INT is a high-volume, efficient passer who is turnover-prone. The net of those two forces, plus the other factors, produces the overall rating.

The starter threshold

The formula is only reliable when a player has enough statistical volume to be meaningful. A QB who threw 3 passes has a meaningless yards-per-attempt figure. Players below the threshold for their position receive a recruiting-anchored fallback rating instead:

PositionMinimum volume required
QB100 passing attempts
RB60 carries
WR / TE20 / 10 receptions
DL / LB / DB10 / 20 / 15 total tackles
K5 field goal makes
P10 punts
OLNo threshold — always uses team-level proxy

The fallback formula: position average + star offset, where the star offsets are −3 (5★), −8 (4★), −15 (3★), −22 (2★), −28 (1★). A 3-star backup on a 70-OVR team averages would rate around 55.

Trajectory and breakout probability

Trajectory (▲▼) is simply the change in overall rating from the prior season. A player trending up has improved production relative to their positional peers.

Breakout probability 🔥 identifies players who are young (FR/SO), have a high recruiting composite (>70/100), but are currently rated below the median for their position group — suggesting they haven't yet hit their ceiling. The probability is a heuristic, not a model output.

Known limitations

What this model does NOT account for (yet)

  • Games played — the CFB Data API doesn't return games_played reliably. We use raw season totals, which slightly disadvantages players who missed games due to injury. EDGE mitigates this by scaling by √(plays counted).
  • Quality of competition for defensive / OL positions — EDGE handles opponent quality for QB/RB/WR/TE via SP+ multiplier. Defensive and OL ratings still use raw counting stats; a DL with 8 sacks in the MAC and in the SEC rate similarly. Opponent-adjusted defensive metrics are planned.
  • Offensive line — individual contribution — no individual OL stats exist in college football data. OL ratings reflect team-level run blocking and pass protection efficiency, spread equally across the unit.
  • Coverage snaps for DB — we count interceptions and PBUs but not targets allowed or yards allowed in coverage. A lockdown corner who never gets targeted will show low counting stats and rate poorly.
  • Receiver targets for WR — EDGE captures production value per reception, but can't distinguish a WR who ran perfect routes but was never targeted from one who was targeted but dropped passes.
  • Transfer adjustment — the model doesn't yet adjust for Year 1 transfer context. Research question RQ9 (Year 1 vs Year 2 transfer performance) is planned.
  • Play attribution gaps — the CFB Data API's play-by-play player name fields aren't populated for every play, which means some plays can't be credited to a specific player. EDGE scores may be slightly underestimated for players on teams with sparse play attribution data.

What's next for the model

The current ratings are descriptive — they summarize what a player did relative to peers in one season. The goal is to make them predictive:

  • Engine B — breakout prediction — a separate XGBoost classifier trained on 2021–2024 data to predict which players will break out next season, using lagged features (recruiting composite, EDGE trajectory, age, snap share change, scheme continuity).
  • Opponent-adjusted defensive metrics — extend EDGE-style opponent weighting to DL/LB/DB using opposing offense SP+ ratings.
  • Transfer portal ROI model — identify which types of transfers (position, class year, conference, EDGE trajectory) produce above-expected Year 1 ratings (Research Question 9/10).
  • NIL valuation vs EDGE — compare On3 NIL estimates against EDGE scores to surface undervalued and overvalued players in the market.

Data sources: CFB Data API (stats, games, play-by-play, SP+, teams, transfers), 247Sports (recruiting composite). Model version: v3.0-edge. Last run: 2025 season.
